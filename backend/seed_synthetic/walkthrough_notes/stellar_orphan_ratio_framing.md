# Walkthrough framing — Stellar's 22% orphan ratio is the demo's pitch, not a defect

When the Part 6 walkthrough lands on the Stellar Events demo tenant, the
operator dashboard will show **9 orphan complaints out of 40 total =
22.5%**. The operator running the demo MUST frame this as the system
working, not the system breaking.

## Why the ratio is high

Stellar's orphan count (9) comes from realistic event-vertical churn:

  - 1 runner walked off mid-event holding 4 open backstage tickets
  - 1 AV lead moved to a competitor holding 2 open complaints (1
    already escalated to L1)
  - 1 catering lead quit mid-Worli-wedding holding 2 open complaints
    (1 already escalated to L2 sr_event_manager)
  - 1 pyrotechnics contractor's licence lapsed holding 1 open urgent job

These are the kind of departures the event industry sees constantly.
The dashboard reads 22% because Stellar's *total* complaint base is
smaller (40 — event ops generates lumpy not steady-state volume); the
same 9 absolute orphans on Greenwood's 120-complaint base would read
just 7.5%.

## Demo script — framing language

When the orphan tile appears on the Stellar dashboard:

> **Say something like:** "Look at this — 22% of Stellar's complaints
> are flagged as orphaned, meaning the original assignee left the
> company while the work was still open. In most event companies these
> just disappear — the runner walks off, nobody picks up the radio,
> and the bride or the client finds out something didn't happen.
> AIBuildCare surfaces them so the operations head can see exactly
> what was left undone, by whom, and at what escalation level. The
> system makes the chaos visible. That's the product."

## What NOT to say

  - ❌ "There's a known issue with orphan handling…" — sounds defensive
  - ❌ "We're working on automatic reassignment…" — true but not yet
  - ❌ "Let me explain the ratio…" — apology framing

## Compare with Greenwood to anchor the story

After showing Stellar at 22%, switch to Greenwood (housing) at 6.7%:

> "Compare with Greenwood — a residential society where staff turnover
> is low. There the orphan rate is under 7%, dominated by one departed
> plumber who left mid-job. The ratio itself tells you something about
> the vertical — high-churn industries get high orphan rates and need
> to see them; low-churn industries get low rates and the few outliers
> stand out clearly."

This framing turns the Stellar number into PROOF that the system is
working correctly — high churn vertical, high orphan visibility, high
operator value.
